### [BODY COPY — Centre and right columns]

Relative UK newcomers Maverick has a refreshingly passionate attitude toward its ever-increasing range of guitars. In little over a year the original F-1, which launched the company alongside the Mockingbird-inspired X-1 and B-1 bass, has been joined by the Streetfighter and Streetfighter Custom Series, an expanded X series, and now the F-2 and F-3.

Maverick's belief in its products seems to be justified too. For a modestly sized outfit it has had considerable impact in a highly competitive section of the market, something which is apparently translating into sales in the country's music stores. It seems the pointy headstock rock guitar may not be the short-lived aberration some thought it would be.

Clearly Maverick doesn't think so - the two guitars here represent a expansion of the company's portfolio and are aimed at consolidating its position as rock guitar manufacturers of the moment and, in the case of the F-2, at attracting a slightly more trad-conscious customer.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**Body and Neck**

Designed by respected guitar builder Trevor Wilkinson, both bodies are sculpted from well-seasoned American alder (as opposed to the basswood used for the F-1). The F-2 is finished in an attractive tobacco sunburst and the F-3 comes in retina-scorching yellow.

The American maple necks are among the slimmest we've seen in a long while. At only 17mm thick at the first fret and 20mm at the twelfth, fears about the stability of such a svelte piece of timber withstanding the considerable pull of a set of strings are understandable, but unjustified. For the solid bubinga stripe running the length of back of the neck is more than just a by-product of truss rod insertion. By gently pressing down the front pickup, it's possible to see that the bubinga is flush with the bottom of the fingerboard.

That substantial chunk of wood, with its opposing grain, is part of Maverick's design to strengthen this slinky neck. The other factor, and one to which the company attaches great importance, is the use of a two-way truss rod. This isn't an original idea but it is used here with great effect to provide extra rigidity and also greater control in setting neck relief. Both guitars also proudly bear grade A Indian rosewood fingerboards (which sport very flat radii) fitted with twenty-four highly polished jumbo frets over their 25 1/2" scale length.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**Hardware**

Of the two, the metal-esque F-3 is perhaps the most typical of Maverick's output thus far. Its Floyd Rose licensed trem is of stamped, as opposed to cast, construction as this is seen as superior by Maverick, and is set up to give lots of upward wang as well enough dive-bomb potential to have the strings hanging off the neck. The locking nut is perfectly set for an almost impossibly low action and, should you need a fret dress at some point in the future, there is a removable Gotoh shim to take it even lower. ### [BODY COPY — Continuation from page 1]

Seymour Duncan Designed Alnico 5 units. The 5-way switch operates to coax out the widest range of tones from them by putting each humbucker in parallel when used in the 'out of phase' in-between positions. The other switch positions work as expected by selecting each pickup in turn.

The F-2 is designed to appeal to a less aggressive player, something that's reflected in the use of covered pickups that are voiced differently to those on the F-3. They're still Duncan Designed but the lack of a third pickup means the 5-way switch is available for a different variation of settings. The two extreme positions kick in bridge and neck units, while the middle position brings in both together - knock the switch one click in from the rear position and you get the bridge humbucker in parallel, and one notch in from the front gives you the outer coils of each pickup out of phase.

Not being bound by the peculiarities of a trem system, the nut on the F-2 is a simple graphite-based type that's been cut extremely well and, given that the strings follow a perfectly straight path from the bridge to the Maverick-branded Gotoh machineheads, tuning problems are very unlikely on this guitar.

The beefy, thru-body strung bridge takes a Strat-style approach to string height and intonation adjustment, so it's simplicity itself to tweak - and should add some muscle to the tonal response of the F-2 too.

Both guitars feature cleverly recessed strap buttons (which allows the retention of the sharply pointed top horn without compromising the strength of the attachment), and also the most idiosyncratic feature of Maverick guitars - the Evolution volume and tone control system. Visually intriguing yet surprisingly practical, the 'roller' effect is achieved by side-mounting the pots inside the body.

### [SECTION HEADER]

**In Use**

The F-3 exhibits the expected rock leanings when plugged in with the powerful, but not overblown, bridge humbucker having a poky upper mid-range that soaks up large helpings of distortion with ease. It seems this particular pickup has been voiced with just that kind of sound in mind, which makes sense, but this means that crunchy overdrive and clean sounds leave a little to be desired. Then again, that's what the following three positions are for. The combination of a parallel-wired humbucker with a single coil isn't a very common option, but one we're definitely in favour of. Brighter than the series version, and definitely more jangly rhythm friendly, it's certainly a surprising and versatile inclusion. The single-coil on its own gives the most precise, direct sound on this guitar, giving a quality that may encourage exploration of this often underused position.

The neck humbucker and middle pickup combination is as twangy as you'd want it to be, with a rounded depth and enough 'quack' to make those Knopfler riffs sound authentic. The last position of the quintet - that of the neck humbucker wired in series - can often be a letdown on guitars of this type, but thankfully the output has been tamed enough to produce a pickup that's just as happy with a generous amount of overdrive as it is with a pristine clean sound. And as such it supplies a very nice fruity tone.

If the F-3 takes some of its cues from Ibanez, then the F-2 has learnt a lesson or two from Paul Reed Smith. Based around similar humbuckers to its sibling, but with chrome covers and a slightly reduced output, it's interesting to note the differences that the alternative hardware makes on essentially the same guitar.

The fixed bridge is obviously a defining factor - something that's apparent before you even switch an amp on. A bigger, warmer tone, long, smooth sustain and greater string separation are all benefits of this solid fixing, obviously

### [SIDEBAR: NECKS TO NOTHING — Centre, brown header box]

**Necks to Nothing**

The supremely slim neck on these Maverick guitars means playability is a breeze the whole length of the fingerboard. All the effort involved employing the dual action truss rod and the strengthening centre section of bubinga, would have been spoilt if the neck terminated with a bulky neck/body join. Clumpy neck joints can be a fly-in-the-ointment of otherwise excellent bolt-neck guitars, rendering the top frets virtual no-go areas. Thankfully the join of these guitars is well thought out, and fits the shape of the hand easily right up to the very last fret.

---

### [SIDEBAR: EVOLUTION REVOLUTION — Bottom right, brown header box]

**Evolution Revolution**

The Maverick Evolution volume and tone system involves more than just sideways-mounted knobs. An average potentiometer as used in a guitar's volume knob is of a logarithmic type, with a 'full-on' to 'full-off' rotation of 270 degrees. The type now used in preference has a special linear response that has characteristics similar to a bypass capacitor-fitted regular pot, giving less tone loss as the volume is reduced. The rotation is shorter, at 180 degrees, which means the travel needed to change from one extreme to the other is covered by one smooth movement.

The pickups take advantage of this starting point by forgoing the midrange focus of the F-3 and the result is a broader, more sophisticated tone. That does mean that there is less upper cut on hand, but what you gain on the swings, you lose on the roundabouts. The alternative selections are equally classy - we do miss the opportunity to knock back the volume of the neck pickup, in the 'both pickups on' position, but Maverick compensates us with the PRS-like position four, with its deep and clear outer single-coils - one of our favourite clean tones.

The lower output and hollower sound of the bridge humbucker in parallel has obvious possibilities as a dirty rhythm tone, but also has enough hard edge when you clean up to allow you to get down with your bad self and get funky. Man.

The under-the-fingers feel of these two fine guitars is almost identical. The locking system does make the string tension differ, but the extremely slim neck profile and ridiculously low action makes both supremely easy to play. The intonation is as damn near perfect as you're going to get and despite the fact that the strings practically touch the frets, both play pretty cleanly up the whole length of the fingerboard. There is a touch of fretbuzz around the first few frets of the F-3 on the low E string, but many of us would be raising the action a notch or two anyway.

### [CONCLUSION BOX — Bottom left]

**Conclusion**

Guitars displaying the styling of these Mavericks were once referred to in certain circles as 'processor friendly'. Which was often a polite way of saying they were a bit bland and somewhat characterless in terms of tone and were best served with a rack's worth of devices to provide some interest. True to the Darwinian spirit however, the concept has evolved and a stronger, more adaptable genus has evolved. These two guitars are prime examples of that species. The original idea of the 'Superstrat' has been refined, and in this case enhanced, with an unprecedented versatility. We only hope the popular conception of guitars of this type won't limit their well-deserved appeal.

### [SIDEBAR: IN ROD WE TRUSS — Centre top, brown header box]

**In Rod We Truss**

Truss rods are designed to combat the pull of the strings on the neck. Regular designs have a metal rod attached to one end of the neck, while the other has one of a variety of nut and thread arrangements. This enables the tension of the strings to be balanced against the resulting bend in the neck to allow a slight 'dip' in the middle. This is important in achieving a good action. The system used here employs two stacked truss rods, working in the conventional manner when adjusted clockwise, and exerting the force in the opposite direction should you turn it anti-clockwise. This gives the option of using very light strings where string tension alone is not enough to gain the correct amount of relief.

---

### [SIDEBAR: WHAMMY THE HAMSTER — Bottom right, brown header box]

**Whammy the Hamster**

Floyd Rose created THE clamping tremolo system, and most of today's units are variations on his design, usually licensing aspects of his patented formula. The F-3's trem is no exception but does have a touch or two of its own identity to add to the sum of parts. The rubber O-rings on the arm not only look good but add much welcome grip to your moments of pitch-bending madness. The Trevor Wilkinson-designed push-in nylon collar also addresses the problem of 'floppy bar,' where heavy usage causes a loose fit and much irritation.
