### [BODY COPY — Left and centre columns]

IF WE ARE TO BELIEVE THOSE in the know, rock is far from dead. Stateside, electric guitar sales have been enjoying a renaissance for the past 18 months and even in the UK the guitar-drought – in the aftermath of Brit-pop – seems to be receding. That's certainly the positive feedback from Maverick Guitars. This new brand burst onto the scene in late 2000 and already is reporting great interest in its stylish mini-range that currently comprises the two guitars on review here, plus the B-1 bass.

Maverick is the brainchild of experienced UK guitar salesmen Barry Stock and Mark James. Having worked for numerous brands in the UK, they wanted to go out on their own and enlisted the help of the UK's resident design guru Trev Wilkinson. As he explains: "Barry and Mark came to me and said 'we'd like to start a guitar company. We've come up with a name, and in keeping with its dictionary definition – a person of independent or unorthodox views – we'd like it to be something a little different. Got any ideas?'" Ideas are Wilkinson's forte and with considerable input from Barry and Mark, the F-1 and X-1 were born. The guitars are made in Korea and each one is quality controlled here in the UK.

Sounds simple? Well, almost...

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**F-1**

Unlike the retro or vintage-based designs (Italia and Fret-King, for example) that have previously flowed from the drawing board of Trev Wilkinson, Maverick is altogether more modern; its start-point hailing from the late eighties when brands like Ibanez exploded at the height of virtuoso widdle-rock. The F-1 follows the near-generic design of the archetypal axe of that period: aggressive, pointy styling, twin humbuckers, 24-fret bolt-on 'speed' neck and double-locking Floyd Rose-licensed vibrato. But although the sales of this style of guitar have certainly slowed since that late eighties pre-grunge boom, brands like Ibanez, ESP, Jackson, BC Rich et al have never gone away. Why did the embryonic Maverick think it had a chance with this style of guitar?

"If you put thought into, and target, your product, you'll find niches that the big boys ignore," defends Wilkinson. "An Ibanez RG550, to me, is still a popular guitar but it's slowed down tremendously. I don't think Ibanez have moved it forward: why don't they do something with the RG that makes people say, 'Wow! That's different'. So that's where we started."

Although Fender's Jazzmaster used similar, sunken roller controls, this particular idea goes back to the early eighties when Trev Wilkinson was in California working with Wayne Charvel. "We'd experimented on a couple of guitars with this type of roller control. But we didn't take it anywhere back then," remembers Wilkinson. "I thought it would be a good place to start with these new designs and bring that idea into the 21st Century."

The idea is simple enough. Housed in the rear-placed control cavity, two conventional pots with large and quite weighty knurled knobs are mounted within a metal surround that lays the pots sideways instead of upright. The carefully shaped scoops on the front of the guitar allow you to easily push the controls away from the playing position, which increases either the volume or applies the brightest tone setting. Initially, it's a little strange because we're used to the clockwise travel of a pot, which, when mounted conventionally, means that you're pulling up on the control, towards you – the opposite of the Maverick's rollers. But perhaps the most important aspect is that once set, it's difficult, in regular playing position, to accidentally knock the positions of the controls. If you regularly tweak your volume and tone for the smallest tonal subtly you probably won't like this feature; but for the style of these guitars, we suspect the leave-it-alone school of tonal tweakery is spot-on.

Aside from these controls then, there is little else that is of note. That's not meant to be disparaging, it's just that in other respects the F-1 is straightforward and perfectly suited to its genre.

The bolt-on neck, which is fixed to the body via four recessed screws on a nicely rounded heel, is hard rock maple – the eight-degree back-angled six-in-a-line headstock is spliced on to the main neck section below the first fret. The main section of the neck is reinforced with a central 13mm wide bubinga strip as the neck itself is extremely thin in depth. At 17mm thick at the 1st fret and 19.8mm at the 12th, it's as near-as-dammit identical to Ibanez's Wizard neck (which measures 17mm at the first fret, 20mm at the 12th fret). It also follows virtually the same width: 43.4mm at the nut 56.8mm at the 24th fret. The flat-backed shallow oval feel will be familiar too if you've ever experienced the Ibanez neck. So, if you play primarily with a thumb-behind left-hand position you'll feel at home here – if you wrap your thumb around, you may be wondering where the hell your neck has gone!

The rosewood board, with stylish 3mm pearloid face dots and clear white side dots, has a pretty flat 406mm (16-inch) radius which matches the pre-set camber of the bridge saddles. The fretting, of a slightly smaller gauge than you'll find on a Wizard neck, is pretty neat and tidy – a little extra rounding to the fret-ends would improve things further but for this price you can't complain.

The vibrato, which is back-routed to allow plenty of upbend, is of the low-profile design and places the string locking bolts directly behind the saddle rather than under the fine-tuners. You'll note also that, in typical Wilkinson style, the arm push-fits into a swing tension adjustable collar – much nicer than the screw-down collar that so many Floyd designs use. And at the end of the 6.35mm diameter arm there are six black rubber O-rings, to add a little grip without resorting to a bulkier knob – a stylistic no-no on a Floyd-type whammy.

Save for the roller controls, electronically the F-1 is straightforward. Two ceramic-loaded, open-coiled humbuckers direct mount to the body with height adjustment made possible by rubber cushion-pads under each pickup. However, the three-way toggle selector – its tip is black-chromed with a couple more of those O-rings – may be a little close to the playing position for any of you with a wayward picking style. Along with the well-placed roller control positioning, any required changes are easily in reach of your picking hand. The output jack is mounted on a metal plate on the side of the guitar.

Overall it's a very detailed, thoughtful design. Even the strap buttons are recessed into the upper horn and base of the guitar; a small point, but these details add time (and money) to the guitar's finishing which, incidentally, is immaculate.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**Sounds**

The F-1 dishes out a fairly predictable small, quite dry and percussive tone when shown the input of a clean amp. In this 'naked' state the pickups sound quite wide and not over-midrange heavy. Sustain is noticeably short and there's not a lot of character. However, as is so often the case with this style of guitar, when you heap on the gain the guitar comes to life. The pickups are not over-powerful but in these days where the most modest start-up amp has gain to spare, you'll soon be riffin' an a shreddin' with this young beast.

Using both a Marshall AVT20 with a 2 x 12" extension cab and a Mesa/Boogie Nomad combo, the almost neutral tone of the guitar enables a host of high-gain sounds. The sonic difference between the two pickups is nicely distinct: the more aggressive bridge humbucker contrasts the softer attack and fuller body of the neck pickup which is ideal for those, er, sensitive moments or high-fret excursions where the bridge pickup can get a little ear-wearing. Lower-fret riffing can become a little indistinct, depending on the amount of gain you're using, but its bite should satisfy most players. The dual-pickup position adds a more hollowed, janglier voice to the guitar – ideal for tonal contrasts and effected passages before you slam into one or other of the solo 'buckers.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**X-1**

In the same way that Wilkinson's retro designs draw on elements of classic guitars and turn them into something new, albeit vaguely familiar, the X-1 does the same but from a more modern start-point. The X-1 shouts BC Rich Mockingbird, yet comparing the two designs side-by-side you'll see that the lower, long horn on the X-1 is in-turning whereas the Mockingbird's lower horn splays out, away from the neck. However, bearing in mind the number of more original designs that have leapt from Wilkinson's drawing board, this has to be classed as a little unoriginal.

With the exception of the body outline, which presents a bulky and heavier aspect to the overall feel of the X-1, the only other differences between this and the F-1 are the reversed headstock and different pickups. Featuring a dozen large alnico rod magnets, the pair of humbuckers here look like a Tom Anderson pickup or perhaps two pairs of modern single-coils fused together. Like the F-1's pickups they are, apparently, fairly off-the-shelf Korean-made designs that Maverick feel suit the guitars.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**Sounds**

Apart from the difference in strapped on feel, the X-1 is not only a bigger-bodied beast but it has a marginally bigger sound too. The pickups seem to have a slightly higher output and with it sound a little darker in the high-end with more midrange push. As you heap on the gain, the differences between the two guitars, tonally, is harder to gauge. However, the differences in tone and feel do add up – you should check both designs before you select your soulmate.

Straight from the box, both guitars played really well. If this light-gauge low-action, thin-necked design is your style then Maverick won't disappoint. Likewise the Floyd Rose vibrato: provided you're happy with the locking aspects and are okay with string stretching and tuning, you'll enjoy the wide-travel in-tuneness of these excellent systems.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**Verdict**

Neither of these guitars brings anything specifically new or innovative to the market. But both display numerous flourishes that add up to a fresh twist on what is – save for paint jobs and body outlines – quite a rigid formula. The sideways-mount roller volume and tone controls are a neat visual idea. While they remove the conventional protruding controls from the playing area, the concept doesn't change the way in which the guitars function, like, for example, an onboard push-button solo boost would.

These tidily built, detailed guitars make Maverick a more than viable alternative to the wealth of more established brands. The guitars are conceived and designed in the UK so maybe, if this style of guitar lights your wick, it's time to invest in what little remains of our home grown, and talented, guitar industry. A fine debut.

*Dave Burrluck*
