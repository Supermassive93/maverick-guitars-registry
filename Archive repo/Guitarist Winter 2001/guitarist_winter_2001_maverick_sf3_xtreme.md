### [BODY COPY — Left and centre columns]

Maverick is rapidly becoming a major player on the mid-price rock electric stage, thanks in part to the excellent F and X Series but also because of their stubborn reluctance to rest on their laurels.

These two brand new models – radically different though they may be – are both aimed at higher price points than their predecessors, but still retain the basic Maverick ethos of high quality, value for money rock guitars of relevance to any player with a love of all things heavy.

Both guitars are technically described as being made in Korea, but this is only half the story, as Maverick's Mark James explains: "Basically, the higher the retail of the guitar, the higher the percentage of the construction is carried out here in the UK. For both the X-Treme and SF-3, the bodies and necks are made in the Far East and final assembly takes place at our Maverick workshop. For example, we'll receive a shipment of SF-3 bodies and neck blanks and all the fretting, dressing, wiring, set up and so on is completed by ourselves. As an illustration, I'd say that around 30 per cent of the X-Treme's production is done in UK, while with the SF-3 it's more in the region of 50 per cent."

"Obviously, we have some pretty stringent quality control systems in place, but we're trying to offer an instrument of a very high quality while maintaining the best possible value. To be honest, the DTI would probably allow us to stamp each SF-3 and X-Treme with the 'Made In UK' legend, but we have no desire to mislead people. We have chosen not to use the larger factories and their use of CNC manufacturing. We use a small factory in Korea that employs around 30 craftsmen and utilise their years of hand-building skills."

Both guitars feature Maverick's eye-catching 'Evolution' horizontal pots, reverse headstocks and the series of rubber 'O' rings around the tip of the vibrato arm and switch caps. But aside from these common features, both have vastly different specs.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**SF-3**

The body shape here is exactly the same as that of the F Series, down to the front and back contours and cutaway shaping, but the wood is swamp ash. Vastly different from standard or northern ash, the wood possesses an attractive open grain – perfectly shown off by the SF-3's honeyburst finish – and what experts agree to be a very well balanced tone. Maverick has located a stock of what they describe as 'special reserve' swamp ash, which is used exclusively for the SF-3. The body is solid and comprises two almost perfectly book-matched pieces.

The natural resonance of swamp ash has been taken into consideration with the choice of pickups here. Both are DiMarzio zebra-coil Airbuckers, with an Air Zone at the bridge and an Air Norton in the neck position. Airbuckers possess a typically low string pull courtesy of a specifically designed magnetic character, which allows for extra sustain of both single notes and chords.

DiMarzio describe the Air Zone as, "a great match for very hot amps, allowing the player to take full advantage of the amp's massive gain without turning the sound to mud or fuzzy noise. Think ultra-fat PAF sound with more output." This would seem to be just the ticket and, along with the warm soupy Air Norton, this should be one good sounding guitar.

Maverick have used a five-way lever switch for selection duties, with the second and fourth positions selecting only the black coil of the bridge and neck humbuckers respectively, making single-coil tones a welcome option. The dimensions of the Canadian maple neck have certainly been arrived at with the rock player in mind. The width of 43mm is standard stuff for a locking nut but, with a depth of just a squeak under 20mm at the first fret, proceedings are thin. In fact, we were concerned that it was almost too thin, and thus unstable, but Maverick have already thought of that. Both necks here feature a three-piece laminate construction with a centre bubinga strip flanked by two outer pieces of Canadian maple. Also, they feature a double-action truss rod which allows both concave and convex adjustment of the neck.

The fretboard, fashioned from, apparently, increasingly scarce grade AA Indian rosewood, is entirely devoid of fret markers aside from a single dot at the 12th fret. As cool as this idea undoubtedly is, the lack of guides is usually tempered by the provision of side markers along the 'board's top edge. Not here though, and this is our only niggle with the SF-3's design. There is a series of black dots round the back of the neck, but they lie a good 12mm from the centre of the board's top edge and are all but obscured when playing the guitar sitting down. There is over 100 years' worth of playing experience working on Guitarist, and each of us got confused without the fret markers at least once during our respective sessions with the guitar. Fortunately, this scenario isn't translated to playing standing up, as the change of body angle alters sufficiently for the markers to be entirely apparent, but maybe a slight rethink is in order.

**SOUNDS:** Even from the outset it's plain to hear that this is a great-sounding guitar. The natural 'brraang' of the swamp ash body combines with the superb definition offered by the DiMarzios to absolutely sing. And no matter how much gain is heaped on – and we're talking about a Boogie Rectifier at full blast here – each string comes through perfectly.

The action was a little too low for our tastes, but it was a matter of mere minutes to get things just how we wanted it, and the slim neck and big frets allowed for all manner of widdlesome phrases to be pulled off with consummate case.

The single-coil options give a stark contrast to the all-out fervour of the humbucker settings. The bridge single-coil gives a tone that sits somewhere between a Telecaster and a Strat, while the neck is almost Gretschy in nature, providing a nice honk along with the crystalline high mids, proving that the benefits of swamp ash aren't confined to the rock arena.

The vibrato works perfectly as well, from subtle wobbles to all out dive-bombs, and the innovative controls require no mental gear changing. All in all, this is a versatile and very well made electric.

### [SECTION HEADER]

**X-Treme**

While this is shape guitar straight from Bernie Rico's drawing board, there are two obvious features that stand out.

The first is the quite staggering fingerboard, equally and diagonally divided between Indian rosewood and Canadian maple. The design, dubbed the 'MatriX', has only been seen on a small number of one-off custom guitars and probably never on a production instrument. The join between the two tonewoods is indistinguishable on the playing surface as the final smoothing is of the highest quality. And if fingerboard wood has any discernible effect on the guitar's tone, as purists would have us believe, the mix here could be interesting.

With a body of solid American alder sporting a flawless metallic paint job, a three-way switch and the usual pair of Evolution pots, the major cosmetic concession is the series of grooves both behind the bridge and between the Gotoh tuners. The idea stemmed from 'experimentation' by one of the Maverick men – suitably 'refreshed' one night – with an old body and a wood screw!

At 42.5mm, the neck is just slimmer than that of the SF-3 at the nut, but has the same skinny depth and is suitably flat at 406mm (16-inch radius). Coupled with 24 medium jumbo frets and, thankfully, the full complement of dot markers (albeit spread across the 'board) this is a rock neck straight out of the box and just what you'd hope for.

Although the pickups aren't name models, both are handwound to Maverick's exact specs and utilise Alnico V magnets. The bridge unit has been overwound in order to provide extra output and both feature a row of screw and flat polepieces.

**SOUNDS:** There is a subtle but definite difference in character between the two pickups due to the bridge HB's undoubted extra heat, and both cope admirably with the big sound provided by the huge cubic amount of alder.

The tone isn't as precise as that of the SF-3, but for rock at least, the X-Treme more than lives up to its name. Our personal tastes again required a quick raising of the action but the neck here is arguably more speedy than its honeyburst counterpart. The frets are beautifully crowned and the actual playing experience is a dream.

Versatility is not really intended to be the name of the game here and the neck pickup tends to be a touch scratchy on clean settings. There's not a great deal of character elsewhere either but, as we've said, the X-Treme is designed to rock and there's little doubt as to its ability to shine in that sphere at least.

---

### [SECTION HEADER]

**Verdict**

These two very different guitars do nothing if not cement Maverick's reputation as a manufacturer that's on the way up. Both the X-Treme and SF-3 are very well put together, and the latter especially could hold its own against any similarly priced instrument from a more famous stable.

The X-Treme rocks like the proverbial mutha and does little else, while the SF-3 possesses different voices that would be suitable to a much wider selection of playing styles.

Both necks are almost unfeasibly thin, so if old Gibson baseball bats are more your thing you may want to look elsewhere. However, for the rest of us, the SF-3 would certainly make for a great main guitar, while the X-Treme combines the best of BC Rich and Ibanez with a few personal innovations.

We have no problem with the price of either instrument – the SF3, at £999, includes a hard case – so dive in, but keep your eyes open.
