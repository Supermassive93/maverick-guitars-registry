### [BODY COPY]

**A Maverick? There goes the neighbourhood…**

For a firm that's only been going eight years, Maverick has one hell of a reputation. Ever since the original Maverick F-1 hit the streets in 2000, we've come to regard these guitars as the most fun you can have with two humbuckers, an overdrive stack and a deserted postal district. So, when Maverick's Mark James told us he'd tweaked the F-1's design to make it even more mental, TG's reaction was a strange mixture of excitement and fear. Better insert those earplugs – this could get a little rough!

**Does the F-1 have decent materials?**

Too right! The spec sheet reads like a 'who's who' of the tree world. First off, you've got a solid basswood body that's contoured by hand and made of timbers carefully selected and stored in a humidity-controlled environment. This is not your average Korean-built rock guitar, dude.

While the original F-1's neck was made of three separate chunks of maple, this axe's bolt-on item is fashioned from a single piece that's been aged in a kiln for a year to sweeten its tone. It's typical of Maverick's idealistic ethos when it comes to construction, and the kind of touch you'd normally expect to find on a swanky custom model. Finally, there's a AAA Indian rosewood fretboard stretching as far as the eye can see. Smashing!

**Talk me through the rest…**

A cursory glance at the F-1's bridge will have the metal fraternity hooting like chimps on heat. Yep, this guitar features a Floyd Rose licensed tremolo and is therefore your ticket to extreme bends, screaming divebombs and all that fun stuff.

Since its appearance in the 1980s, the Floyd Rose concept has brought joy to countless hairy men across the planet. As a principle it's simple, but devastatingly effective. By locking the strings in place at the nut and bridge, the floating trem lets you yank at the F-1's dual-feel whammy bar like a horny teenager when his parents are out – without suffering the tuning slips that early wobblers such as Jimi Hendrix had to contend with. Admittedly, this kind of trem can be a headache for uninitiated players to set up, but once in position it will only take the odd tweak of the bridge tuners to keep everything ticking over. Lazy and cackhanded? Then you should go for the fixed bridge version, which also weighs in at £499.

**How does it play?**

Like sliding down a greasy pole. This axe is almost effortless to play and these Maverick guys really know their onions when it comes to building the perfect snarling metal beast. For instance, with 24 frets lined up on the fingerboard, Maverick have included generous cutaways at the top and bottom, giving you unbeatable access to the upper echelons without expense to the neck join. To ensure the guitar is comfortable for both stage and sofa, you'll find a rib contour round the back that fitted TG's chest like a, er, glove. And just when you think things couldn't get any peachier you will notice how slim the F-1's neck profile is. Maverick reckon it's the fastest around, and anyone who heard our rendition of Surfing With The Alien wouldn't argue with them.

**What's with the tone controls?**

We thought you'd never ask! The F-1's patented Evolution tone controls are probably the best example of this guitar straying from a traditional electric guitar blueprint and coming up smelling of roses. Like the pickup selector and strap buttons, these roller controls are recessed into the body; not only making them look damn cool, but also stopping you basting them during frenzied windmill sessions. Style over substance? Maybe so, but they'll make a great conversation-starter with that nymphomaniac in the front row.

### [SECTION HEADER — Centre column]

**LORD OF THE STRINGS** — CHANGING STRINGS ON A FLOATING TREMOLO

Some 20 years after its inception the Floyd Rose tremolo still commands fear and respect in equal measure. We know all about the benefits: the outrageous scope for whammy abuse, the divebombs and the showboating. Yet somehow the floating trem unit has become a byword for hassle and faff; a time-consuming trimming that stands between you and the act of rocking out. Perhaps the most common slander of all is that changing strings on this type of tremolo takes the best part of an afternoon. Let's straighten that one out, right now.

Using an Allen key (you should get one free with your guitar), unlock the, er, locking top nut. Next, detune the strings (so that they don't ping off and leave you wearing an eyepatch) before using the Allen key to loosen the bolt securing the retaining block (1) in the bridge (one for each string). Now cut the strings. If you change your strings regularly, consider buying a tool like the Planet Waves Pro-Winder (a cutter, winder and stretcher all for £8.95). Now remove the old strings.

Your next job is to take your new string and cut it about 50mm from the ballend (2). Place the end you've just cut in the bridge and use the Allen key to tighten the block back up then thread the other end up the neck and through the locking nut. Cut each string about 2-inches past the machinehead, feed it through the hole, and tighten it to pitch by turning the machinehead (3). After stretching the strings and returning them several times (to ensure stability of pitch), tighten the locking top nut and, if you think necessary, use the bridge microtuners to make any further tuning tweaks.

### [SECTION HEADER — Right column]

**OK, so how does it sound?**

When Maverick first arrived on the scene the muscular punch of its pickups got TG all sweaty and excited. We weren't the only ones, either. By the time the word had spread, half the Download bill had taken pliers to their guitars and crammed in a pair of Maverick humbuckers. Mark James, for instance, has nailed a sound that sits poised between clarity and warmth. He could tell us how he did it, but then he'd have to kill us.

With that in mind it's no surprise that the Maverick 'A' series humbuckers deliver a superbly powerful projection. There's some great mid to be coaxed from the bridge pickup and the tone has enough body to let single notes fill the room before fading, but you'll never find your riffs growing too thick or muffled. Maverick have reworked the copper windings in the neck humbucker too, adding more beef to deliver a bluesier tone than the original F-1. Ease off the gain, meanwhile, and you'll be hit by the authority of the Maverick's unadulterated tone. It's warm and funky, and nowhere near as flat as the clean performance delivered by some 'metal' axes out there.

**Come on then, is it Mastercard time?**

We think so. For £499, the Maverick F-1 is an individual axe which stands out in the vast forest of pointy wannabes. And once you plug it in and hang it round your neck you'll see exactly what all the fuss is about. At some point soon Maverick is going to snag a superstar endorsee that will send the price of these axes skyrocketing. Until that day comes you owe it to yourself to try this superb guitar. We couldn't give an F-1 about anything else…
