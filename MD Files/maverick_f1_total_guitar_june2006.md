### [SECTION: THE SIZZLE — Body copy, left column]

In the nicest possible way, you might describe Maverick founder Mark James as slightly obsessive. We don't mean he has to switch the light on and off three times before he leaves the house. Nor do we mean that he flies into a rage if you mess up his colour-coded cutlery drawer. The nature of Mark's obsession becomes obvious as soon as you pick up the phone to him. It's the electric guitar. He won't rest until he's made the best one the world has ever seen.

Mind you, it doesn't hurt to be a bit obsessive when you're trying to get a new guitar company off the ground and into the headlines. And over the past nine years, that's exactly what Mark has done. TG regulars might already be familiar with the story of Maverick Guitars. The seeds of this British firm were sown in 1997, when a meeting between Mark and his designer friend Trevor Wilkinson led to the decision to buy a custom workshop in Korea. From this base, the fledgling company began shredding the rulebook and drawing up their own gameplan.

Essentially, Maverick are one of a handful of modern luthiers who appreciate the genius of the '50s designs, but feel the time has now come to push things forward. Right from the start, the Maverick mission statement has been that no guitar design is perfect, and everything can be improved upon. "If you want to be safe," Mark told one guitar magazine in the early days, "then play something else."

### [SECTION: WHAT'S IT GOT? — Centre and right columns]

Both models remain in production to this day, but with Mark constantly evolving the F-1 as fresh brainwaves strike him, this is hardly the same animal that rocked our world six years ago. And so, it falls to TG to decide whether Mark has created the ultimate electric guitar with this latest incarnation – or whether he should be told to put his hands behind his back and stop bloody fiddling.

Call us shallow, but when the F-1 burst out of the blocks in 1999, the first thing TG noticed was how bloody cool it looked. This wasn't the kind of guitar you'd buy as a teenage thrash-metaller, then mothball in the attic when your balls dropped and your tastes matured. It was a guitar for life; borrowing lightly and judiciously from design principles of the past, while adding its own unique touches. Some of the features – and we're thinking specifically of the Evolution roller controls here – made girls want to shag you. Others – like the speedy neck profile and ergonomic body contouring – made you play better. Short of actually having tits, it couldn't have been much sexier.

In the same way that you wouldn't 'tidy up' the Mona Lisa, it's a relief to find that Mark hasn't revised the cosmetics of the F-1. You've still got the double cutaways, the purposeful-looking headstock and those space-age rolling dials (recessed into the body for maximum efficiency). The body still dips and dives like a rollercoaster, and the metallic finish still implies that this guitar costs more than £499.

Neither is the beauty skin deep. Tonewoods are critical to the performance of any electric guitar – playing a part in everything from weight to amplified sound – and plenty of thought has gone into choosing the best ones for the F-1. Maverick have timberyard contacts around the world, supplying anything from alder to korina, but with this particular model, they've settled on Canadian basswood for the body and rock maple for the bolt-on neck.

There's good logic behind both choices. Canada is famous for the quality of its rock maple – it's where most of the big boys get their necks – and there's no doubting the strength and bright tone of this material. Basswood, meanwhile, is a more neutral-sounding timber which Mark describes as a "halfway house between mahogany and alder". Seeing as Gibson guitars tend to use mahogany, while Fender often favour alder, we're hoping that the F-1's tone might deliver the best of both worlds.

Maverick's famous attention to detail is also evident when it comes to the fretboard. Most firms stain their rosewood fretboards for cosmetic reasons, which can make them look shiny and cheap after you've played them for a couple of years. By contrast, the F-1's fingerboard simply uses premium quality AAA Indian rosewood, and leaves it at that. Finished with classic dot inlays (and without any fussy binding), it looks great and feels even better.

### [INTERVIEW — Within TOP GUN box]

*How did you come up with the idea for the unusual rolling volume and tone knobs on your guitars?*

"Well, Fender had done something similar with their Jaguar guitars in the 60s. I thought, 'This is so simple. Why was it applied to a guitar in the mid-60s and why was it put on the upper bout of the guitar where it wasn't actually convenient to use?' So, it started this process of evolution, thinking about how we could control the guitar's volume and tone in a much more ergonomic fashion. What we settled on, is the fourth evolution. And that's what we've fitted to our guitars since 1999."

*What makes Maverick different from other guitar companies?*

"I just want to make the best guitars that I possibly can. I'm not interested in selling loads of guitars and making lots of money. I don't want a Lamborghini. It would just make me a big-headed twat! I think Maverick guitars are totally fucking cool. I truly believe that."

*A recent post on Total Guitar's forum asked, 'Whatever happened to Maverick?' What's your answer to that?*

"We did disappear for a while, but that was because of stuff that was going on in the background. Now, I'm in total control and building Maverick back up, one step at a time. I'm in this for the long haul."

The maple neck is now a one-piece item (this hasn't always been the case) which has been kiln-dried and finished by hand with a shallow C profile. Mark tells us that he designed it with speed in mind, and the presence of two octaves and great upper fret access suggest there should be plenty of scope for widdling. It's also interesting to note that the scale length is 25 inches on the nose (as opposed to the standard 'Fender' scale of 25.5" or the typical 'Gibson' scale of 24.75"). We'll have to see if this has any effect on the F-1's playability once we get it on the lap.

Then there's the LP-1 Floyd Rose tremolo. To their credit, Maverick have realised that this configuration won't be everyone's cup of tea (it's traditionally a pain in the arse to set up, a hassle when it comes to changing strings and a disruption to palm-muting), and it's also possible to get the F-1 in a hard tail format (the F-1 HT – also priced at £499, or £529 for the left-hand version). That said, it seems to TG that the floating trem option doesn't cost any more, and will help technicians get the most from this model, so it probably makes sense to go with it.

Besides which, TG is intrigued to see if this unit is as good as Maverick claim. Mark has sweated bullets to get the LP-1 absolutely right, and he tells us that the fact it's made of tempered steel – rather than the cast-metal used by most companies – brings it closer to the original unit that Eddie Van Halen abused on his Kramer in the 1980s. We're also told that it should give a cleaner tone and greater stability – something we'll be looking out for when we plug in.

### [SECTION: BUILT TO LAST? — Left column]

**BUILT TO LAST?**

The best thing about Maverick is that they haven't become the kind of faceless corporation that's more concerned with maintaining the sales trajectory than the quality of the product. This is Mark's business, but it's also his passion, and that's very apparent when you cast a critical eye over the build of the F-1. The neck is one of the toughest we've seen, managing to combine superb top fret access with real stability. The fretboard we've touched on already; it's professional and finished with great care. All the body joins and contours are flawless, and the 16:1 machineheads are totally solid (though you won't use them much once the Floyd Rose is set up). Our critical scalpels are poised, but there's almost nothing to stick the knife into here.

### [SECTION: HANDS ON — Centre column]

**HANDS ON**

It's not often TG gets the chance to say this about a metal guitar, but the F-1 is actually pretty comfortable. For starters, it's not too heavy, and it offers a weight distribution that stops the headstock plummeting and keeps the fingerboard exactly where you need it. Sitting down with this guitar is a treat too, thanks to the familiar feel of the lower body and the presence of an upper rib contour. TG also appreciated the fact that Maverick have recessed all the buttons and knobs, making for a smooth playing experience and less chance of accidentally selecting the wrong pickup.

It's nice that the F-1 is comfortable and everything, but what really knocked TG out was the sheer playability of the neck. Within moments of picking this model up, the slim profile seemed to fit in the palm like it belonged there, and while that means you won't get quite the same sense of satisfaction as you might from chugging on a Les Paul, there's no doubting the potential for finger gymnastics. All the tricks of the metal trade – from tapping to alternate picking – are catered for here, and the floating trem unit is one of the easiest and most responsive we've used.

Of course, none of this would be worth a damn if the F-1 sounded like a bag of cats being swung against a wall. It doesn't. Starting at the neck humbucker on a clean amp setting, TG was struck by the mellow glide and richness of the tone; nothing like the lifeless dirge you'll get from some 'rawk' guitars before you pile on the gain.

That said, these pickups are desperate to break up, and even when you're playing on the clean channel, striking the strings hard brings in a more ragged tone.

As such, it seemed only fair to let the F-1 off the leash with a walk on the wild side. When it comes to playing hard, we felt the low end 'oomph' of the neck humbucker was the most convincing setting. It's unbelievably full and powerful, like a warm slap in the face. There's a strong argument for the middle position too; it's got the beef of the neck, but with added edge. And the bridge? Well – that's the clincher. Dial up the gain and you've got a truly searing tone that'd put fire into the belly of even the most simple pentatonic solos.

Alongside all that, there's a quirkiness to the F-1's tone that makes it more memorable than other axes we've played recently. Sometimes, for instance, you'll accidentally squeeze out notes that sound exactly like a Strat. At other times, you'll be reminded of the fiery thickness of a Les Paul. It all adds up to a guitar with character in abundance.

---

### [SECTION: THE BOTTOM LINE — Right column]

**THE BOTTOM LINE**

The weird thing about reviewing the F-1 is that you'll occasionally glance at the price tag and remember this guitar only costs £499. It feels like the kind of model that should be pushing past the grand mark and much of this is down to the love that has obviously been invested at every stage of its development. The construction is as superb as we've come to expect from Maverick. The cosmetics are just as blinding as ever. And – if anything – the tone and feel of this flagship model are even better than they were upon its launch. It's 'F'-ing fantastic.
